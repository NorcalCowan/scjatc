Introduction

Introduction

This plan is designed to be a general “All Hazards Plan” and outlines some recommended steps that can be taken to prepare your facility, staff and family for a variety of possible disasters. This is just a plan; the execution of the plan is the responsibility of the user. This plan is based upon Mitigation, Preparedness, Response and Recovery phases. The objective of this plan is to produce a sense of confidence in the users that they have the resources and practices in place to respond and recover from an emergency / disaster as effectively as possible.

Annual reviews and drills of this plan should be conducted by all parties the plan as a whole should be reviewed and updated annually.

Note: The JATC staff indicated that during their operation, Staff and or Faculty are always present on this site during operation. All parties and contractors should be aware of this plan and the actions/requirements that may be needed by each of them during an event.

General Information

General Information

Purpose

This Emergency Action Plan, hereafter referred to as the “Plan”, establishes policy, defines personnel responsibilities and provides guidance for the response to, mitigation of, and recovery from the potential consequences of an emergency situation occurring at The Northern California Sound and Communication JATC site in San Leandro, California. Emergency situations include significant accidents, incidents, events, or natural phenomena, which could seriously degrade the safety or security of the site. This Plan develops a framework for the development, implementation, and maintenance of an Emergency Plan relevant to activities/operations conducted at the JATC. Situation-specific guidelines contained within this plan will provide facility personnel with the details of their duties and responsibilities during an emergency.

This plan was developed using information from CAL-OSHA EMERGENCY ACTION PLAN Title 8 Section 3220 Fire Prevention Plan Title 8 Section 3221 to establish guides and procedures to mitigate the potential consequences of an emergency/disaster that threatens the health and safety of the JATC staff, students and visiting personnel.

Scope

This plan applies to all personnel temporarily or permanently assigned to or visiting the JATC facility in San Leandro California. This Plan addresses them JATC Emergency Management Program as it relates to potential emergencies that could arise at the JATC San Leandro site.

Concept of Operations

This JATC site will utilize an All-Hazards approach to emergency management based upon the identified hazards. The risk assessment process included the identification of potential hazards, including Natural hazards, Technological-caused hazards and Human-caused hazards. The likelihood of their occurrence and the vulnerability of people, property and the environment were also considered. The potential events that have been identified as the planning basis for this plan include, but are not limited to earthquakes, severe weather / power outages, structure fires, flooding, bomb threats, civil disorders, terrorist/Active intruder incidents. The JATC does not store hazardous chemicals over nationally recognized threshold planning quantities (TPQs).

During emergency situations, the JATC concept of operations is based on centralized authority maintained by the JATC Emergency Coordinator with decentralized responsibilities coordinated through the facility staff and the Emergency Response Team members.

Mitigation

Mitigation

Mitigation Definition and Overview

Mitigation is defined as proactive action taken to reduce or eliminate the adverse effects of natural and man-made hazards on people and property.

The goal of MITIGATION is to decrease or eliminate the need for response as opposed to simply increasing response capability. Mitigation begins by conducting a site hazard assessment, which requires a self-appraisal of major areas effecting site safety. These areas include geographic location of buildings, proximity of potential hazards such as waterways, availability of transportation for possible evacuation, structural integrity of buildings, etc.

Hazard vs Risk

The terms "hazard" and “risk" are often used interchangeably in the context of mitigation. Hazards and risks are identified through vulnerability analysis, consequence modeling, code/regulation compliance, quantitative risk assessment and historical data correlations.

Risk is the product of potential consequences and the expected frequency of occurrence. The basic concept of acceptable risk is the maximum level of damage to the building that can be tolerated, related to a realistic risk event scenario or probability. Consequences may include death, serious injury, the extent of structural damage, monetary loss, interruption of use, or environmental impact.

Assessing and Addressing Hazards

Mitigation assesses and addresses the safety and integrity of the following types of hazards to minimize or prevent adverse impacts:

  • Facilities – window seals, HVAC systems, building structure
  • Security – functioning locks, controlled access to the facility
  • Threats – probability of natural disasters or accidents
  • Site Environment – social climate on campus

Resources used to assess and address hazards include:

  • Existing safety plans
  • Security and safety-related policies
  • Floor plans of building
  • Maps of local evacuation routes
  • Site crime reports
  • Known safety and security concerns
  • Logs of police calls for service
  • Student and staff handbooks
  • Disciplinary files
  • Local memos of safety concerns

Facilities

Structural and non-structural measures can mitigate the effects of natural hazard incidents.

Structural Mitigation

Includes physical rectification and standards such as

  • Building codes
  • Materials specifications
  • Performance regulations for the construction of new buildings
  • Retrofitting of existing structures to make them more hazard-resistant
  • Protective devices such as retaining walls

California’s frequency of earthquake activity has resulted in sophisticated seismic building codes for all buildings and special inspection requirements, enforced by the state, for school buildings. Building codes establish the minimum standards for safety.

The construction of hazard-resistant structures is perhaps the most cost-effective mitigation measure. Hazard mitigation in existing structures is generally more costly, but when carried out effectively before a disaster, prevents loss of life, reduces damage and avoids the outlay of associated costs for response and recovery operations.

Non-Structural Mitigation

Typically concentrates on the securing of light fixtures to ceilings, installation of wind shutters, strapping or bolting generators to walls, and numerous other techniques to prevent injuries and allow for the continued use of the site.

Site occupants are particularly vulnerable to non-structural damage. Excessive sway in any building may cause damage to non-structural components such as hung lath and plaster ceilings, partitions, water pipes, ductwork, electrical conduits, and communication lines. Storage units, filing cabinets and library shelving shift or fall if not properly braced.

Although students and staff may duck under desks and be safe from falling objects, ceiling components that drop in hallways and stairs can make movement difficult, particularly if combined with power failure and loss of lights. Additional falling hazards that are common in sites are wall-mounted televisions or ceiling mounted projectors.

Security

A higher level of protection is appropriate for facilities that will enhance site recovery. A hazard assessment should assure that the site buildings have functioning locks and controlled access.

Threats

Mitigation requires assessment of local threats, including the probability of industrial accidents and natural disasters. Threat assessment considers potential hazards in the neighborhood such as

  • High voltage power lines
  • Facilities containing toxic, chemically reactive and/or radioactive materials
  • Transportation routes of trucks and trains carrying hazardous materials
  • Underground gas and oil pipelines
  • Underground utility vaults
  • Above-ground transformers
  • Multi-story buildings vulnerable to damage or collapse
  • Water towers and tanks

Since location is a key factor in determining the risks associated with natural hazards, land use plans are a valuable tool in identifying areas that are most vulnerable to the impacts of natural hazards such as wildfires, earthquakes and flooding.

Earthquake

A simple seismic evaluation that focuses specifically on educational facilities is rapid visual screening. This method is intended as an initial step in identifying hazardous buildings and their deficiencies. It is most useful for large school districts by providing an economical preliminary evaluation of the seismic risks, thereby reducing the number of buildings that require a more detailed evaluation by a structural engineer.

The visual survey of a building can be completed in less than 30 minutes and can be accomplished from the street without benefit of entry into a building. However, hazardous details may not be visible and seismically dangerous structures may not be identified as such. Nonstructural interior components are not evaluated.

Fire

Of the many hazards that can endanger an educational facility and its service to the community, the most prevalent is fire. Design against fire has long been built into state building codes, in the form of approved materials, fire-resistant assemblies, exiting requirements, the width and design of stairs, the dimensions of corridors, fire suppression systems, and many other standards.

Severe Storms / Flooding

This is the most common natural hazard in the United States, affecting over 20,000 local jurisdictions and representing more than 70 percent of Presidential Disaster Declarations.

Factors that can affect the frequency and severity of flooding and the resultant types of damage include:

  • Channel obstructions due to fallen trees
  • Accumulated debris and ice jams
  • Culvert openings that are insufficient to move floodwaters
  • Erosion of shorelines and stream banks
  • Deposition of sediment that is carried inland by wave action
  • Dam and levee failure

Calling 911

911 is a telephone number dedicated to calls for emergency assistance, such as a traffic accident, fires, paramedic service, crime in progress, bodily injury, imminent threat to life, or major property damage or loss.

The 911 Dispatcher is a trained dispatcher who will prompt what additional information is needed, based on the type of emergency (e.g., what the scene looks like, number of injuries or deaths). In order to complete an assessment on the telephone, the 911 Dispatcher may have many questions to ask depending upon the nature of the problem. It is very important for the dispatcher to obtain as much information as possible, in the interests of responder safety and to dispatch the correct level of medical response.

The 911 Dispatcher may ask you to stay on the line until responders arrive. This will assist the dispatcher in keeping the fire, police or rescue squad updated as the situation being reported unfolds.

When Reporting an Emergency

  • Remain calm and speak slowly and clearly while explaining the type of emergency you are reporting
  • Provide your name, location of the incident and your location, if different from the scene of the emergency

    • Note: Though the technology exists which presents address and phone number data immediately to the 911 Dispatcher it is not available in all locations. The 911 Operator is trained to confirm and verify the phone number and address for EVERY call received. The dispatcher will ask questions concerning the type of emergency being reported. The answers you provide will be relayed to the responding emergency personnel.

  • Answer all questions asked by the 911 Dispatcher, even those that seem repetitious
  • Stay on the line while the 911 Dispatcher processes the call. You may need to provide additional information or to receive instructions from the 911 Dispatcher. Do not hang up until the Dispatcher says to do so

When Using a Mobile (Cellular) Phone

911 calls from cellular phones are answered by a local agency. It is important to know your cell phone number and to be aware of your surroundings so you can tell the dispatcher where you are. Ideally, street names and addresses should be provided. Since many cell phone calls are made from moving vehicles, callers should inform the dispatcher when they change locations.

Time is of the essence. Every 911 Operator knows that. It hampers response when a 911 operator has to ask the caller for information several times over because he/she couldn't comprehend what was being said. Try to be as calm as possible and speak slowly and clearly. This ensures the 911 operator has the correct information the first time he/she asks for it.

Establishing Reliable Communication

Establishing reliable communication networks is critical for dealing effectively with an emergency or crisis. Timely contact with law enforcement and other public agencies is necessary for effective response. Site staff members and students must be told what is happening and what to do. Families of students and staff members must be informed about the situation, including the status of their student and or family member. Rumors must be quelled. Lastly, providing proper communication with the media if they arrive on your site is important.

Terminology

One of the most important things to do is use common / simple terminology. Response agencies will communicate more effectively with site if similar roles are described with similar wording.

Technology

During an emergency, telephones and cell phones will only be used to report emergency conditions or to request emergency assistance. In the event that telephone and/or electrical service are interrupted, other means must be relied upon to relay information. A bullhorn and/or runners will be utilized within the site building or site grounds.

Communication Methods

Standard Telephones

A standard telephone plugged into a standard jack may work even when the electrical power is off. Designate a telephone number as a recorded “hot line” for family to call for information during emergencies. The goal is to keep other telephone lines free for communication with first responders and others.

Mobile Telephones

These phones may be the only method working when electrical service is out; they are useful to administrators and staff who may be in route to or from a site.

Intercom System

Ideally, systems should include instructor-initiated communications with the office using a handset rather than a wall-mounted speaker.

Bullhorns and Megaphones

A battery-powered bullhorn or megaphone should be part of the site’s emergency toolbox to address students and staff who are assembling outside the site. Procedures governing storage and use will help ensure readiness for use.

Two-way Radios

Two-way radios provide a reliable method of communication between rooms and building at a single site. Train all staff fully to operate the two-way radio.

Computers

A wireless laptop computer may be used for communication both within the facility and to other sites. E-mail may be a useful tool for updating information for staff. Post information such as site evacuation, closure or relocation on the home page of the JATC website.

Fax Machines

Possible uses include off-site accidents where lists of students and staff members involved, their locations, and needed telephone numbers can be quickly and accurately communicated. Medical information, forms, and authorizations can be faxed and returned in emergencies.

Media Communications

Most news people are sensitive when reporting emergencies that occur in school settings and are interested in doing a reputable job.

The following suggestions will promote clear communications with the media:

  • Designate one area (on or off site) where media briefings can take place
  • Instruct all staff to refer all information and questions to the designated person (Remind staff that only designated spokespeople are authorized to talk with news media)
  • Insist that reporters respect the privacy rights of students and staff. Answer questions within the limits of confidentiality
  • Take initiative with news media and let them know what is or is not known about the situation
  • Speak to reporters in plain English
  • If the emergency is a death, Principal should consult with the deceased student/staff member’s family before making a statement about the victim
  • If there is involvement with a criminal case, work in conjunction with law enforcement spokesperson
  • When communicating, maintain a unified position and message; keep messages concise, clear, and consistent
  • Don’t try to “kill” a story; don’t say “no comment;” don’t speculate; don’t pass blame
  • Delay releasing information until facts are verified and the sites position is clear
  • Assign sufficient staff to handle phones and keep a log of calls and personal contacts
  • Express appreciation to all persons who helped handle the emergency
  • Always provide a phone number to call for additional or updated information

Response Team Lead

The Response Team Lead directs on-scene operations and is responsible for overall management of the incident. Coordinates the actions of the team, interface with First Responders, and provide updates to the ownership/representative.

The Response Team Lead will coordinate the activities of

  • Search and Site Assessment
  • First Aid
  • Student / Staff Liaison
Responsibilities
  • Assess the scene
  • Ensure the safety of students, staff and others on the site
  • Develop strategies for appropriate response
  • Coordinate response efforts
  • Lead by example
Start-Up Actions
  • Assess type and scope of emergency
  • Determine threat to human life and structures
  • Activate the appropriate emergency action
  • If evacuation is necessary, verify that the route and assembly area are safe
    • Direct the opening of the emergency cache
    • Set up the leadership post
    • Obtain personal safety equipment
  • Establish appropriate level of organization of the team(s) needed during the response
Operational Actions
  • Determine the need for and request First Responder assistance
  • Monitor and assess the total site situation
    • View site map periodically for response team progress
    • Check with Team Leads for periodic updates
    • Ensure First aid is available
    • Deploy Search and Site Assessment teams
    • Coordinate the accounting for and support of students and staff with the Student Staff Liaison
    • Maintain communication with all teams during the response using the communication means available to the group
    • Reassign personnel as needed
    • Release staff as appropriate
    • Remain in charge of site until redirected or the event is ended
Deactivation
  • Receive briefing from public safety agency to obtain “All Clear”
  • Authorize deactivation of response teams as they are no longer required
  • Provide input to the After-Action Report
  • Proceed with recovery operations, if necessary

Search and Site Assessment Team (2 members)

Assess all facilities on the property(s) for safety and damage, lead any and all search efforts if needed. Review and execute utility shut-offs if needed. Sweep quickly through the buildings to identify location of trapped or injured students and staff. Notify leadership of those who are trapped and or injured.

This is a two-person team with an identified team leader. Take no action that might endanger you. Do not work beyond expertise. Use appropriate safety gear. Size up the situation first before taking any actions.

Equipment and Supplies
  • ID Vest
  • Work and latex gloves
  • First aid backpack
  • Clipboard with job description and map
  • Fire extinguisher if available
  • Backpack with goggles, flashlight, face masks, pry bar, duct tape
  • Hard hat
  • Whistle with master keys on lanyard
  • Caution tape
  • Chalk marker
Start-Up Actions
  • Create two teams with two searchers in each team
  • Put on personal safety gear
  • Obtain all necessary equipment (i.e. flashlight, marking chalk, first aid backpack)
  • Contact Leadership before beginning Search and maintain communications
  • Establish Search Route and communicate it to Leadership
Operational Actions
  • Before entering a space, evaluate if the area can be entered safely
    • Report structural damage to team leader
    • Use yellow caution tape to barricade hazardous areas
    • Do not enter severely damaged areas
  • One team member remains outside of the area being searched for safety and communication
  • Building search and sweep process to be completed on each floor:
    • Follow established search to cover all areas
    • If area is safe to enter, search assigned area (following map) using orderly pattern
    • If room contains injured or dead persons, mark the outside of door with an “/”
    • Report by phone or radio to the Leadership Post when room or area has cleared
  • Follow directions from leadership post
  • Report gas leaks, fires, or structural damage to Leadership immediately upon discovery
  • Record exact location of damage and triage on map and report to Leadership

Student / Staff Liaison

Ensure the care and safety of all students on the site except those who are in the First Aid Station. Contact and interface with all family and guests on property to ensure, clear communication, status and evacuations if needed.

Start-Up Actions
  • Identify team leader
  • Put on safety vest or position identifier
  • Check in with Leadership for situation briefing
  • If site is evacuating:
    • Verify that the assembly area and routes to it are safe
    • Count students or observe the classrooms as they exit to make sure that all classes evacuate
    • Initiate the set-up of support supplies
Operational Actions
  • Monitor the safety and wellbeing of the students and staff in the Assembly Area
  • If first aid is needed refer to First Aid Station
  • When necessary, provide water and food to students and staff
  • Make arrangements for portable toilets if necessary, ensuring that students and staff wash their hands thoroughly to prevent disease
  • Update records of the number of students and staff in the assembly area (or in the buildings)
  • Account for and document all staff and students prior to them leaving the site
  • Make arrangements to provide shelter for students and staff
Closing Down
  • Return equipment and reusable supplies to building

First Aid Team

Provide first aid support as needed and establish emergency supplies. Establish the First Aid treatment area, triage and emergency medical response/ first aid. Ensure that appropriate actions are taken using basic first aid. Coordinate with the Search and Site Assessment Team and inform Leadership when the situation requires health or medical services that staff cannot provide.

Start-Up Actions
  • Set up First Aid area
  • Obtain and put on personal safety equipment including vests and non-latex or nitrile gloves
  • Secure First aid supplies
Operational Actions
  • Admit injured students/staff to First Aid area, listing name on master log
  • Administer appropriate first aid
  • Keep accurate records of care given
  • Continue to assess victims at regular intervals
  • If and when transport is available, do final assessment and document. Keep and file records for reference—do not send with victim. Determine where the injured will be transported to / location.
  • A copy of the Student’s and Staff Emergency Contact Information must accompany student removed from site to receive advanced medical attention
First Aid Area Actions

Locate triage (injury sorting area) at the entry of the First Aid area. This area is for the injured to be quickly evaluated for severity of injury and directed to the appropriate treatment area.

Immediate Care - For people with life and limb threatening injuries that require immediate attention, such as difficulty breathing, severe bleeding, major burns and shock. Locate immediate care in an area out of sight of most students and staff but accessible to emergency vehicles.

Delayed Care - For injured individuals who do not require attention within the first hour. Such people may have lacerations, broken bones or need medication. Locate near the immediate care area, but shield from the sight of the injured in immediate care area.

Recovery

Recovery

Recovery refers to the follow-up measures taken after a disaster to return the affected site back to normal operating conditions. Recovery focuses on restoring the infrastructure of the school (utilities, phones, water) to service, re-establishing a sense of emotional safety and returning to the learning environment as quickly as possible after a crisis. Recovery actions are generally begun as response activities diminish, but some activities (e.g., keeping track of personnel time) must be started simultaneously with response. Recovery measures are designed to assist students and staff impacted by physical, psychological and emotional trauma following an event.

Impact Assessment

Once the safety and status of staff and students has been assured and emergency conditions have abated, assemble staff to support the restoration of the sites programs. Defining mission-critical operations and staffing will be a starting point for the recovery process.

  • CONDUCT a comprehensive assessment of the event’s physical and operational effects. Assess physical security, data access, and all other critical services (e.g., plumbing, electrical)
  • EXAMINE critical information technology assets and personnel resources and determine the impact on the facility for each asset and resource that is unavailable
  • DOCUMENT damage, lost equipment and resources, and special personnel expenses that will be required for Insurance and perhaps FEMA assistance claims
  • ADMINISTER Workers’ Compensation if claims arise that are on-the-job injuries
  • ARRANGE for ongoing status reports during the emergency to
    • Estimate when the site program can be fully operational
    • Identify special facility, equipment, and personnel issues or resources that will speed resumption of classes

Resumption of Training

Re-entry Into the Facility

The site administrator is the individual responsible for authorizing re-entry into the facility. A damage assessment team should:

  • Survey the site after a disaster
  • Report findings to site administrator
  • Ensure that timely and accurate data is received

Re-entry should occur only after the site administrator has been assured by public safety officials and / or local contractors that the safety of the site has been restored.

Relocation

In the event the site is damaged to the extent that all or a portion of it is uninhabitable until repairs are made, plans must be developed to address the relocation of educational services and staff to alternate facilities until repairs are made or the facility is rebuilt.

Repairs and Construction

Any repairs and rebuilding of damage wrought by site violence or natural disaster must carefully consider the input and feelings of the victims. There is a strong and significant psychological connection to death sites that must run its full course and should never be discounted or ignored. Making changes and reconstruction in and around death and injury sites requires a delicate balance with the need to resume normal activities. The construction must be the result of careful and deliberate consultation with students and staff Action should never be rushed.

Requirements of JATC for Training Staff

Before implementing the emergency action plan, the employer shall designate and train a sufficient number of persons to assist in the safe and orderly emergency evacuation of employees.

The employer shall advise each employee of his / her responsibility under the plan at the following times:

  • Initially when the plan is developed
  • Whenever the employee's responsibilities or designated actions under the plan change
  • Whenever the plan is changed

The employer shall review with each employee upon initial assignment those parts of the plan, which the employee must know to protect the employee in the event of an emergency. The written plan shall be kept at the workplace and made available for employee review. For those employers with 10 or fewer employees the plan may be communicated orally to employees and the employer need not maintain a written plan.

Note: Authority and reference cited: Section 142.3, Labor Code.

Security Information

Security Information

This facility is monitored by a series of video surveillance cameras both interior and exterior – these cameras are recorded and the video is maintained for a minimum period of time approximately 6 months.

There are multiple live monitoring stations available and this is a web based system so it can be accessed from multiple locations if desired.

The site also maintains a building alarm system that is monitored from an external source/ vendor.

The building access system is both hard key and electronic access key card. Both of these systems should be reviewed bi-annually for changes in users and potential access threat elimination. The assignment of these access keys is monitored by the administrative staff.

Vendors and contractors who are providing services to the facility are provided a temporary access key card to perform those services. These keys are available at the main reception desk and are verified and signed out to those individuals.

Limiting Building Access

This is an educational facility with students and staff accessing multiple locations within the site during normal operating hours. A limited number of access doors should be available during business hours for the students and staff to move freely about the site. These doors should be monitored by staff on regular basis to assure access is not being compromised by others without the authority to be onsite.

All doors to classrooms, labs, offices, storage areas and gathering locations should be secured when not in use.

Regular security / visibility walks should be performed by the staff throughout the course of the normal operating day.

During non-regular operating hours, weekends, and evening classes all access points to the facility should be limited and controlled to maintain building security.

Before securing the facility after operations, perform a walk-thru of the facility. Each area should be visually secured and doors should be locked.

Site Security Assessments

Quarterly, a designated individual should perform a Site Security Assessment review. This review should include, but is not limited to:

  • Outside security / parking lot lighting
  • Security camera functionality
  • Perimeter fencing check
  • Door locking functionality

Threat Reporting

All threats should be taken seriously and reported. If a threat is imminent, call 911 immediately.

The JATC requires employees to report all threats or incidents of violent behavior which they observe or are informed about to the JATC site administrator. The site administrator should take the steps necessary to complete a “Threat Incident Report” as quickly as possible, including a private interview of the victim(s) and witness (s). The report will be used by the JATC to assess the safety of the workplace and to decide upon a plan of action. Elements of the “Threat Incident Report” and any subsequent actions relating to the incident should be recorded in a tracking system for use by the site.

The tracking system as well as all investigative files should be kept secure and maintained separately from other records.
